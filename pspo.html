1. Scrum Theory — Empiricism

Scrum is founded on empiricism and lean thinking. Empiricism asserts that knowledge comes from experience and decisions should be based on what is observed.

Three pillars of empirical process control:
• Transparency — The emergent process and work must be visible to those performing the work and those receiving it.
• Inspection — Scrum artifacts and progress must be inspected frequently and diligently to detect variances.
• Adaptation — When inspection reveals deviation beyond acceptable limits, adjustment must happen as soon as possible.

Empiricism vs Defined Process Control:
Defined process control works when all inputs are known and outcomes are predictable. Scrum uses empiricism for complex work where requirements and solutions evolve unpredictably.

Five Scrum Values: Commitment · Focus · Openness · Respect · Courage
These values give direction to work and actions. When embodied by the Scrum Team, the pillars of Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation come to life.

2. Scrum Team — Roles & Accountabilities

The Scrum Team consists of one Product Owner, one Scrum Master, and Developers. It is a cohesive unit of professionals focused on one objective at a time: the Product Goal.

Product Owner — accountable for:
• Maximizing the value of the product
• Developing and explicitly communicating the Product Goal
• Creating and ordering Product Backlog items
• Ensuring the Product Backlog is transparent and understood
The PO is one person, not a committee. Their decisions must be respected by the whole organization.

Scrum Master — accountable for:
• Scrum being established and understood
• Serving the Product Owner (PBL management, stakeholder collaboration)
• Serving the Developers (self-management, focus, removing impediments)
• Serving the organization (leading Scrum adoption)

Developers — accountable for:
• Creating a plan for the Sprint (Sprint Backlog)
• Instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done
• Adapting the Sprint Backlog daily toward the Sprint Goal
• Holding each other accountable as professionals

Teams are self-managing (decide who does what, when, and how) and cross-functional (have all skills to create value each Sprint). Optimal size: 10 or fewer people.

3. Scrum Events

All events are opportunities to inspect and adapt. Events have maximum timeboxes.

The Sprint — the heartbeat. Fixed length ≤ 1 month. A new Sprint starts immediately after the previous. No changes that endanger the Sprint Goal. Only the PO can cancel a Sprint (if the goal becomes obsolete).

Sprint Planning (max 8h / month) — answers 3 questions:
• Why is this Sprint valuable? → Sprint Goal
• What can be done this Sprint? → selected PBIs
• How will the work get done? → initial plan, tasks

Daily Scrum (15 min, same time/place) — for Developers only. Inspects progress toward Sprint Goal and adapts the Sprint Backlog. The format is up to the Developers.

Sprint Review (max 4h / month) — inspect outcome of the Sprint; determine future adaptations. Scrum Team + stakeholders. Results presented; progress toward Product Goal discussed. The PO invites stakeholders.

Sprint Retrospective (max 3h / month) — for the Scrum Team to inspect itself: individuals, interactions, processes, tools, Definition of Done. Identify improvements to implement in the next Sprint.

4. Scrum Artifacts & Commitments

Product Backlog (commitment: Product Goal)
An emergent, ordered list of everything needed to improve the product. Never complete. Items at the top are more refined. The PO is accountable for ordering. Refinement is an ongoing activity.

Sprint Backlog (commitment: Sprint Goal)
Composed of: Sprint Goal (why) + selected PBIs (what) + a plan for delivering them (how). Created by and for the Developers. Updated throughout the Sprint. Highly visible real-time picture of progress.

Increment (commitment: Definition of Done)
A concrete stepping stone toward the Product Goal. Must meet the Definition of Done. Multiple Increments may be created within a Sprint. A Done Increment may be released at any point — the PO decides.

Definition of Done
A formal description of the state the Increment must meet. If not an organizational standard, the Scrum Team creates it. Items not meeting DoD return to the Product Backlog. DoD creates transparency and is shared across all teams working on the same product.

Definition of Done vs Acceptance Criteria
DoD applies to all Increments (quality floor). Acceptance Criteria are specific to individual PBIs (what "done" means for that item). A PBI must meet both its acceptance criteria AND the DoD.

5. Product Vision & Strategy

A product vision describes the long-term purpose and direction of the product. It is not mandated by Scrum but is a critical complement to the Scrum framework for Product Owners.

The vision provides context for all decisions — what to build, what to reject, and how to order the Product Backlog. It is the "north star" that guides the Product Goal.

Product Goal (Scrum Guide 2020)
The Product Goal is the long-term objective for the Scrum Team. It is in the Product Backlog. The Scrum Team must fulfill (or abandon) one Product Goal before taking on the next. Product Goals are stepping stones toward the vision.

Product Roadmap (complementary practice)
A communication tool that shows how the product is expected to evolve over time. Not prescribed by Scrum. Updated as learning occurs. Owned by the Product Owner.

The PO must balance short-term delivery (Sprint Goals) with medium-term targets (Product Goals) and long-term direction (product vision).

6. Product Value

The Product Owner is accountable for maximizing the value of the product resulting from the Scrum Team's work. The Scrum Guide does not prescribe how to measure value — it depends on context.

Outcome vs Output:
• Output = features delivered, story points, velocity
• Outcome = user adoption, revenue, NPS, problem solved
Focus on outcomes, not outputs. Value is only realized when Increments reach users.

Product Backlog ordering factors:
Value · Risk · Dependencies · Learning · Cost of delay · Alignment with Product Goal

ROI & value measurement:
The PO should understand the financial and strategic return of features. Higher-value, lower-effort items should generally appear higher in the Product Backlog — but this is never mechanical.

Value is delivered when a Done Increment reaches users — not when a Sprint ends or a backlog item is marked complete.

7. Product Backlog Management

Product Backlog Refinement — the ongoing act of breaking down and further defining PBIs into smaller, more precise items. The Scrum Guide does not specify a timebox. Developers who will do the work collaborate with the PO.

Ordering, not prioritizing
The Scrum Guide uses "ordering" deliberately. The sequence reflects value, risk, learning, dependencies — not a single priority score. Items higher in the backlog are more refined and ready.

Readiness (informal concept):
A PBI is "ready" when it is small enough to complete in one Sprint and well enough understood by the Developers. Scrum does not mandate a formal Definition of Ready.

Estimation:
Developers size the PBIs (they do the work). The PO understands estimates to make trade-off decisions. Estimation format (story points, hours, T-shirt sizes) is chosen by the team.

Emergency items:
New urgent work can be added to the Product Backlog at any time. The PO orders it appropriately. During a Sprint, the PO collaborates with Developers on scope changes within the Sprint.

8. Stakeholder Management

The Product Owner is the primary interface between the Scrum Team and stakeholders. They represent stakeholders but are not their delegate — they make independent ordering decisions in the best interest of the product.

Sprint Review as stakeholder forum:
The Sprint Review is the primary touchpoint for stakeholders. The PO invites relevant stakeholders. Feedback from the Sprint Review informs the next Product Backlog updates.

Key PO responsibilities toward stakeholders:
• Understand and translate stakeholder needs into PBIs
• Communicate the Product Goal and progress
• Manage conflicting stakeholder interests through Product Backlog ordering
• Protect the team from ad-hoc requests that bypass the Product Backlog

Stakeholders & Product Backlog changes:
Anyone wanting to change the Product Backlog must convince the Product Owner. Stakeholders cannot directly modify the Product Backlog.

The Scrum Master helps the PO by coaching effective stakeholder collaboration techniques.

9. Release Management

When can an Increment be released?
At any point during or after a Sprint, as long as the Increment meets the Definition of Done. The decision to release belongs to the Product Owner. Release decisions are not restricted to Sprint boundaries.

Potentially releasable Increment:
Any Done Increment is potentially releasable. "Potentially releasable" means it has met all quality standards (DoD) and could be in the hands of users. The PO decides if and when to release.

Continuous Integration / Continuous Delivery (CI/CD):
Not prescribed by Scrum. CI/CD helps teams maintain a working, tested codebase throughout the Sprint, reducing integration risk and making Done Increments more trustworthy. Recommended complementary practice.

Undone work:
Work that does not meet the Definition of Done cannot be released and cannot be presented as complete at the Sprint Review. It returns to the Product Backlog.

Technical debt:
Accumulates when teams cut corners on quality. The PO should understand its impact on future velocity and value delivery. Technical debt items belong in the Product Backlog with appropriate ordering.

10. Scrum in Practice

Scaling with Nexus:
Nexus is Scrum.org's scaling framework for 3–9 Scrum Teams working on a single Product Backlog. It extends Scrum minimally, adding a Nexus Integration Team and Nexus-level events. One Product Owner manages the single Product Backlog.

Multiple teams on one product:
Even with many teams, there is one Product Owner and one Product Backlog. This ensures consistency in ordering and alignment with the Product Goal.

Sprint Goal flexibility:
The Sprint Goal is fixed for the duration of the Sprint. The Sprint Backlog (how and what) can be adapted by Developers as they learn. The Sprint Goal provides flexibility and coherence.

When things go wrong mid-Sprint:
Developers collaborate with the PO to renegotiate the scope of the Sprint Backlog. The Sprint Goal remains intact. Cancellation is only when the Sprint Goal becomes completely obsolete.

Continuous improvement:
Mature Scrum Teams inspect and adapt continuously. The Retrospective is the formal opportunity; Developers also adapt daily. The Definition of Done should be expanded over time as the team matures.
